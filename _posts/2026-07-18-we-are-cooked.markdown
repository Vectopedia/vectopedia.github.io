If you asked me, I would have zero doubt that Generation Alpha's destiny is to be cooked. From MMORPGs, to mass media, to infinite reels of chaos, our attention spans shorten bit by bit. It's only when you grow up and look back at the good old days, you realize "good" probably isn't the wisest term to apply. Millions of kids slam and lock the door, then stay on the screen for millenia, skipping meals and isolating from social life. While game design and content creation are fun, they disclaim consequence caused by addiction, and in case you enjoy being alive and happy, that's never a good thing.

Technology was created as things to help. Of course, with creativity and curiosity comes with consequence, such as misuse and dependence on technology. During the Cognitive Revolution, fire is one of the earliest examples, which likely came from natural accidents. When we domesticated fire and used it to cook, scare, and heat, fire domesticated us as well. Without cooked food, we would starve; without burning torches, the lions would pop our heads off like lollipops; without bonfires, we would fall sick in the cold night. The Agricultural Revolution, then, allowed us for mass plantations, but even hunter-foragers were living healthier lives compared to those peasants farming their backs off, yet we continued because of the fantasy we created to believe that soon we would be stuffed and happy one day. That apparently never happened. Finally, the Industrial Revolution was one of the greatest leap in advancement of Homo sapiens, allowing for efficient mass production and even more starving workers. The same fantasy was created here, with capitalism constantly yelling at us, "Imagine all the money you could earn! You would have a stuffed tummy, a happy family, and any amount of tickets you want to explore the world!" Yet, once again, that never happened. We follow these beliefs even to this day, working with a receding hairline, with a dream and a few bucks to spare.

Even as adolescents, the difficulties are just as congruent. We post on social media, praying for views and likes each day; we play video games at lengthy hours, praying for the burst of pleasure when you finally get past the obstacle that would take you months. We miss out on the social, beautiful, _real_ life, which is the most important aspect in the adolescent stage of cognitive and identity development. It should be the time we take to explore and create; alas, we instead choose to spend time selling our information. The bigwigs behind the stage earn billions out of advertisements and rewards accidentally encountered by oblivious teens. The fruits of our labor, on the other hand, include mostly anxiety and fear of missing out.

When I open the back door of my peers, I see thoughtful, aware, bright, and amazing kids. Yet most of them have suffered from the traps of technological dependence. Without games, they start begging their moms for their iPad; without social media, they isolate themselves in their rooms worrying about their online status; without TVs, they start getting bored out their minds, causing them to pick up the remote again. Parents have a difficult time as well. Generations above often have a lack of information about how these technologies work, generating panic and worry. They begin to worry about online predators, blind eyes, and leaving the dinner table too early just to get back to the game. However, I think I've found a great solution to this chaos.

There's no solid solution for each person– it must be acknowledged that everybody has unique thoughts and reactions. The trick, though, is to solve the problems that lay beneath. Did you know that cutting off a branch of a tree can't stop it from growing new ones? Addicts will grow new branches even if you try to stop them, finding new ways to exploit rules every day. The most effective way to stop that is to _cut down the trunk_! Often, parents worry:
- My kids shouldn't be on the screen all day.
- What about the predators? I've heard of plenty in the news…
- They should go out more and enjoy the sunshine!

Sure, that might work for a week, tops, but soon enough they'll figure out they can scroll in bed or while going out. Truly, we should be asking:
- What are the environmental causes and effects of the addiction?
- Which things online make them feel like home?
- How can I help my child understand that the overuse of technology is unhealthy?

There are many helpful books out there to pave and guide the way for parents and children alike, but I would __strongly__ recommend _The Anxious Generation_, for parents and educators, and _The Amazing Generation_, for children and adolescents, both books written by Jonathan Haidt. I wouldn't stop you from reading both books, no matter of age, though.

According to repeated interviews and observations, it can be seen that a surprising amount of Generation Alphas are fully aware of their addictive misuse of technology, and have attempted at stopping themselves. The majority have failed or given up. Sometimes our actions matter more than how much we know.

So, is our generation cooked?  
Yes, our generation has already been cooked thoroughly.  
That doesn't mean it's too late, though! We can already start influencing our current and future generations. We can break the loop of fantasy of failure before our next generations are doomed.

Good luck, and have fun cutting those trees down.
