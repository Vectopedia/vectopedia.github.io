__We are cooked.__  
In fact, I think Generation Alpha's destiny is to be cooked! We spend hours on MMORPGs, social media, and infinite reels of chaos, shortening our attention spans bit by bit. It's only when you grow up, and you look back at the good old days, you realize that "good" isn't necessarily the best term to apply. Nowadays, children with devices slam and lock the door, isolating themselves in their digital world. They are willing to stay there for millennia, to skip meals, to isolate themselves from social environments, and to never see the sun. I'm not going to lie, though– game design and content creation are certainly fun. However, they also mean you disclaim all consequence caused by their addictiveness. Imagine all of that consequence in those millions of games, posts, and videos. Unless you enjoy feeling alive, online addiction is never desirable. What is technology? How does it affect adolescents? How can we solve the problems caused by addiction? Which resources can help?

# What is technology?

Technology, according to the New Oxford American Dictionary, is "the application of scientific knowledge for practical purposes, especially in industry." They are creative tools to help us solve problems. Though, with creativity and curiosity always comes consequence. People began misusing and depending on technology. The earliest example, fire, was domesticated by us in the Cognitive Revolution; however, fire also domesticated us, for we had a difficult time surviving without them. Without cooked food, we would starve; without bonfires, we would fall sick in the chilly twilight. Next, a very long time later, there came the Agricultural Revolution. Techniques for renewable sources of food were popularized, which were intended to help feed higher populations. Yet, the original hunting-foraging people had a much better and happier life than those farmers in the scorching-hot wheat fields. We prayed that we would no longer starve and have happy lives one day. Spoiler: that never happened! The Industrial Revolution, after yet another long time, came next. Mass production in variety became possible, which was perfect for capitalism and consumerism, expanding them to the entire world. Yet, the same story was told. "_One day,_" capitalism would tell you during your grueling work hours in the sweatshop, "_You will be rich! Just imagine all the delicious food you could eat, the happy family that you've dreamed of, the business you've always wanted to start! You will finally be happy, one day._" But all you had was a dream, a few bucks to spare, and a receding hairline.

# How does it affect adolescents?

Even as adolescents, the difficulties are just as congruent. We post on social media, praying for views and likes each day; we play video games at lengthy hours, praying for the burst of pleasure when you finally get past the obstacle that would take you months. We miss out on the social, beautiful, _real_ life, which is the most important aspect in the adolescent stage of cognitive and identity development. It should be the time we take to explore and create; alas, we instead choose to spend time selling our information. The bigwigs behind the stage earn billions out of advertisements and rewards accidentally encountered by oblivious teens. The fruits of our labor, on the other hand, include mostly anxiety and fear of missing out.

When I open the back door of my peers, I see thoughtful, aware, bright, and amazing kids. Yet most of them have suffered from the traps of technological dependence. Without games, they start begging their moms for their iPad; without social media, they isolate themselves in their rooms worrying about their online status; without TVs, they start getting bored out their minds, causing them to pick up the remote again. Parents have a difficult time as well. Generations above often have a lack of information about how these technologies work, generating panic and worry. They begin to worry about online predators, blind eyes, and leaving the dinner table too early just to get back to the game. But I think I've found a great solution to this chaos.

# How can we solve the problems caused by addiction?

There's no solid solution for each person– it must be acknowledged that everybody has unique thoughts and reactions. The trick, though, is to solve the problems that lay beneath. Cutting off the branches of a tree will not stop it from growing new ones. Addicts will grow new branches even if you try to stop them, finding new ways to exploit rules every day. The most effective way, instead, is to _cut down the trunk_! Often, parents worry:
- My kids shouldn't be on the screen all day.
- What about the predators? I've heard of plenty in the news…
- They should go out more and enjoy the sunshine!

And then they try to find answers to those questions, then enforce them as rules. Sure, that might work for a week, tops, but, soon enough, they'll realize they can just scroll on socials in bed or play games while going out. Rather, we should be asking:
- What are the environmental causes and effects of the addiction?
- Which things online make them feel like home?
- How can I help my child understand that the overuse of technology is unhealthy?

Then, finding solutions will take more time, but in the end, there's no need to push the tree to fall– gravity will do the rest of the work.

# Which resources can help?

There are many helpful books out there to pave and guide the way for parents and children alike, but I would __strongly__ recommend _The Anxious Generation_, for parents and educators, and _The Amazing Generation_, for children and adolescents, both books written by Jonathan Haidt. I wouldn't stop you from reading both books, no matter of age, though.

Another good idea is to find a therapist. Not just any psychiatrist you find in a hospital, for medication isn't necessary at all. I'm talking about the people who listen, help, and guide you through crises. For both kids and parents, even just talking about problems can help you relieve the stress and find some answers.

# And finally…

According to repeated interviews and observations, it can be seen that a surprising amount of Generation Alphas are fully aware of their addictive misuse of technology, and have attempted at stopping themselves. The majority have failed or given up. Sometimes our actions matter more than how much we know. If you're a parent, you must understand that addiction is caused by the choice to give in to technology, so leaving also must be intentional. Your kid must also intend on helping themselves in order for them to find a solution that they find useful, and your job isn't to do the same, but is instead to pave and guide the way.

So, is our generation cooked?  
Yes, our generation has already been cooked thoroughly!  
That doesn't mean it's too late, though. We adolescents can already start influencing our current and future generations. One day, we will all be dads and moms, so if we don't figure it out, the next generations will walk the same path to doom. It's our job to stop that.

Good luck, and have fun cutting those trees down.
